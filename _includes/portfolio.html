Neurological Benefits of Curcumin

In addition to fighting inflammation, curcumin has several other benefits including neurological ones. Curcumin is non-toxic and therefore passes through the blood-brain barrier, meaning it can have positive effects in the brain as well as the body.

Curcumin

chemical formula: C₂₁H₂₀O₆

Curcumin is an organic compound made up of only carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen. It's a pure substance, so when it’s broken down it becomes dihydrocurcumin, tetrahydrocurcumin, etc.

Genetic Regulation

Curcumin has been shown to increase docosahexaenoic acid (DHA) in the brain (See: Salem N Jr, Litman B, Kim HY, Gawrisch K. *Mechanisms of action of docosahexaenoic acid in the nervous system*. Lipids. 2001 Sep;36(9):945–59. doi: 10.1007/s11745-001-0805-6. PMID: 11724467). DHA increases cellular phosphatidylserine, which strengthens the cell membrane and helps with cell signaling. Thus, curcumin can aid with brain function.

Bacterial Regulation

By increasing gut eubiotic bacteria such as firmicutes, curcumin has many downstream effects. Firmicutes are associated with short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs), which help reduce stress and anxiety (see: van de Wouw M, Boehme M, Lyte JM, et al. *Short-chain fatty acids: microbial metabolites that alleviate stress-induced brain-gut axis alterations*. J Physiol. 2018 Oct;596(20):4923–4944. doi: 10.1113/JP276431. Epub 2018 Aug 28. PMID: 30066338; PMCID: PMC6187046).

Circadian Rhythm Regulation

Curcumin has trace amounts of melatonin. It also helped the mice used in my study adjust faster to their new time zone when severe circadian rhythm disturbance was induced.
